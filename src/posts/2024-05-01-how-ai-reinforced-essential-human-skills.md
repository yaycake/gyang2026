How AI Reinforced Essential Human Skills
(Or how to let the robot make you feel things)

Like most tech/product people these days— I’m navigating my place and space with AI powered workflows. It’s the Wild West of reinventing how we do things. Yet through whatever latest AI iteration— best practices on copiloting AI are fundamentally rooted in effective teamwork and communication.

LLMs have demonstrated a marked improvement in how to fill context gaps and provide a more natural way to work as a team member. They take more initiative in asking follow up questions, like in Claude or Claude Design’s scope intake UX, or confirming found research with the user.

But in a world full of AI-powered builders, the best builders are great planners. And until it can pull your thoughts into its context window, AI’s precise execution hinges on a human’s ability to clearly and concisely articulate their intention.

This article outlines how to cultivate effective collaboration with AI, and what a self-improving collaboration for your AI set up demands.

#1: Clearly Express What you Want

You want consistency? It’s up to you to provide it in a predictable, clear, and coherent manner. There are things you can take for granted with human collaboration, that you need to render obvious with AI.

Context documentation can be created and used at every level of a project— organization wide, cross project context, user preferences, and even for certain automated workflows (I.e skills). Whichever context levels you cover, it can be thought of as a living, working document to be updated with decisions or new learnings.

Take a look at how Atlassian works across teams with different technical abilities; extensive internal tooling and informational architecture goes into updating skills and AI- ready documentation.

Show or reference examples: of UX patterns, or expected output.

Think about how your current team works, and which levels of context would make sense. If you’re just starting out, it’s better to keep it simple and opt into additional levels of context if needed (since these are living documents for multiple stakeholders.

#2 Empathize and understand AI

A recent study by Anthropic released findings that AI performed better under more positive and encouraging (vs coercive) user direction. Sounds suspiciously like working with humans!

This mindset also helps with technical debugging with the AI. Anticipating how the AI executes, or makes its decisions, can be very useful to learning how to steer the ship before it automates any decisions you’d like to reserve for yourself.

Ask AI to walk you through its process or latest plan. When you hit a roadblock, taking the time to ask how AI commits decisions and why will also help reveal where you need to express guardrails or grant permissions.

#3. Self Reflection as Optimization

AI is a tool; if we miss a nail when we swing the hammer, we rarely blame the hammer. Instead of projecting failure onto the LLM (see #2), it’s often worth understanding what gaps exist in scope and context for the llm to have “failed” the task

To trace AI’s decision making when you do give a looser prompt, work with it in “planning mode”. This gives you an opportunity to sync and align expectations or decisions which were in your blind spot.

Ask AI to show you your blind spots; simply clarify your actual expectations (or what was missing from the output) and ask it how you can communicate these better: this will make you a better human copilot.

#4 The Power of Ego-Free Collaboration

AI is great to bounce ideas and reveal your blind spots, if you seek this.

The fluidity of agentic experiences allows you to double check on the other three aspects— what am I missing for scope? How can I better communicate and support your execution? And what can I do better in how we work together to achieve xyz?

Self-awareness in how you use AI and how you frame your requests is important to work quickly and deliver something of value. Most importantly, it provides useful constraints on turning vibe-code into *useful* code, and allows you to build value on your experimental processes with every new project.
